Towards an Agentic Framework
for Payment Orchestration

Payment execution is more complex, more consequential, and harder to replicate than invoice capture. This paper sets out the architecture of an agentic payment system, what it requires to work reliably, and why replication demands capabilities that take years to build.

Centime Inc. 2025 For internal distribution
Executive Summary

Agentic invoice capture is a meaningful capability. Agentic payment orchestration is a more valuable one — and a harder one to build. The reason is structural: payments span multiple modalities, multiple systems, and multiple failure modes. Each payment is also a financial optimization decision that most AP teams currently make by default rather than by analysis. An agent with access to real-time cash position, payment terms, modality economics, and supplier preferences can make better decisions on every payment, automatically. The barriers to replicating this are not primarily technical. They are the depth of payment stack access required and the entitlement to participate in a client's payment workflow — both of which Centime earns through its bank channel in ways a direct-to-market competitor cannot easily replicate.

Getting into a client's payment workflow is not a procurement decision. It is a trust decision made by a CFO, validated by their bank, and underwritten by their audit posture. The bank channel solves this. Nothing else does it as efficiently.

1. The Problem This Solves

Payments are executed, not optimized

In most mid-market AP operations, payment decisions are made by default. Invoices are paid when someone processes the queue, using whatever payment method is configured for that vendor, with limited awareness of discount windows, cash position, or modality economics. The result is a material and largely invisible cost: missed early pay discounts, suboptimal payment timing, unnecessary wire fees, and working capital tied up in payables that could have been extended.

The complexity is compounded by the diversity of payment types in practice. A single AP run might include domestic ACH, international wires in multiple currencies, virtual card payments, check runs, and early pay program settlements. Each has different rules, cutoff times, cost profiles, failure modes, and remittance requirements. Managing this well requires more information and faster decision-making than most AP teams can provide manually.

An agentic payment system addresses this by treating every payment as an optimization problem — selecting modality, timing execution, routing remittance, confirming settlement, and handling failures — continuously, across the full payables portfolio.

2. Why Payments Are Structurally Complex

Complexity that does not simplify with AI

Invoice capture complexity is largely a data quality and extraction problem — one that LLMs are well suited to reduce. Payment complexity is different in character. It is process complexity rooted in decades of banking infrastructure, regulatory requirements, and failure recovery patterns. Adding an LLM layer does not make it simpler. It makes it more automatable, which is different.

  • Multiple payment modalitiesACH, domestic wire, international wire, virtual card, RTP, check, early pay settlement — each with distinct timing windows, cutoff times, reversal rules, fee structures, and failure modes. Selecting the right modality per payment requires knowing all of them.
  • International paymentsFX rate timing, correspondent banking chains, local payment rails by country, beneficiary bank validation requirements, sanctions screening, regulatory purpose codes, and country-specific reporting obligations. A payment to a supplier in Germany has a materially different process than one in Brazil or Thailand.
  • Remittance deliveryGetting remittance detail to the supplier in a format their AR system can consume for cash application remains one of the most persistently unsolved problems in B2B payments. EDI 820, structured email, PDF advice, and portal posting are all in active use. Mismatched remittance creates reconciliation work on the supplier side that damages relationships.
  • Settlement confirmationInitiating a payment and confirming it arrived are different events, sometimes separated by days. ACH returns can arrive up to two business days after settlement. International wires can be held in correspondent chains. An agent that initiates and forgets has not completed the process.
  • Failure recoveryWhen a payment fails — wrong account number, returned ACH, rejected wire — the recovery process is almost entirely manual today. Diagnose the failure reason, correct the data, resubmit through the right channel. This is exactly the kind of structured, multi-step remediation process an agent can handle reliably.
3. Intelligent Payment Routing

Every payment is an optimization decision

Beyond execution, the agent can optimize across four dimensions simultaneously on every payment. This is where the financial value concentrates — and where the distance from conventional AP automation is greatest.

Dimension 1

Payment timing

Pay on day 28 of net 30 terms — not day 1, not day 31. The agent knows the due date, the current bank balance, and the cash forecast. It preserves float without risking late fees. A human processing a queue does not optimize this. They pay when they reach the invoice.

Dimension 2

Early pay discount capture

Every invoice with early pay terms is evaluated against the effective APR of taking the discount versus the company's cost of capital. The decision is made automatically, before the window closes. Discount windows are consistently missed in manual AP operations — not through negligence but through volume and timing.

Dimension 3

Modality selection

Virtual card on eligible suppliers generates a rebate. ACH on standard terms is lowest cost. Early pay via CentimeCredit generates a spread. Wire is used only when necessary. The agent selects the optimal modality per invoice based on supplier acceptance, amount, timing, and rebate economics — not a blanket policy.

Dimension 4

Working capital sequencing

Across the full payables portfolio, the agent sequences payments to maximise days payable outstanding without triggering late fees or damaging supplier relationships. This is a treasury-level optimisation that most mid-market companies do not perform because it is too complex to execute manually at the invoice level.

These dimensions interact. An invoice might be worth taking the early pay discount on — but only if cash position supports it, and only if the rebate from virtual card does not exceed the early pay discount value. The agent solves a multi-variable optimisation problem on each payment, in real time, against live bank balance data. That requires inputs that only Centime's stack can provide in combination.

4.1 Human authorisation is structural, not a limitation

The architecture retains human sign-off on payment execution. This is not a concession to the current state of the technology — it is the correct design for mid-market finance. Controllers have segregation of duties requirements. Boards have payment authorisation policies. Auditors expect a human in the payment approval chain. An agent that attempted to bypass this would not be deployed, regardless of its accuracy.

The agent's value is in the quality and completeness of what it presents for authorisation — optimal timing, correct modality, rationale visible — not in removing the authorisation step.

7. The Two Barriers to Replication

Technical depth and entitlement compound each other

01
Payment stack depth

Direct bank connectivity, actual payment rail access — ACH origination, wire execution, card issuance — settlement monitoring at the bank level, FX infrastructure, and sanctions screening. This is not achievable through payment processors alone. Most AP software vendors hand off to a processor and lose visibility the moment funds leave. An agent that cannot see what happens post-initiation cannot handle failures, confirm settlement, or optimise timing against real balances. Building this stack takes years and significant capital.

02
Payment workflow entitlement

Being authorised to participate in a client's payment workflow requires someone with authority to have decided: this system moves our money. That is a trust decision made by a CFO, validated by their bank, and embedded in their internal controls. A new entrant with a capable agent framework hits this wall immediately. The bank will not enable payment execution for an unknown vendor. The CFO will not authorise a new system without their bank's endorsement. The auditor will flag it.

A competitor could build the same payment routing logic in months. They cannot obtain payment workflow entitlement without bank relationships — and bank relationships cannot be purchased. They are earned through demonstrated trust, compliance posture, and track record. Centime arrives through the bank. That is the entitlement.

The two barriers compound. A well-funded competitor who builds the technical stack still cannot get into the payment workflow without bank relationships. And bank relationships do not come without the compliance posture, trust, and track record that take years to establish. Neither barrier alone is insurmountable over time. Together they create a window of advantage that is meaningful.

8. An Honest Assessment

Where the limits are

Human authorisation will not be removed

Autonomous payment execution — without human sign-off — is not the near-term opportunity and should not be positioned as such. The value is in optimising what the agent presents for authorisation, not removing authorisation. Mid-market Controllers are not ready to cede payment authority to a system, and their auditors would not permit it if they tried.

International payments require incremental rail build-out

The full international payment optimisation capability described here requires local payment rail access in each target corridor, FX execution capability, and country-specific compliance handling. This is a multi-year build that should be sequenced by corridor volume and customer demand rather than attempted all at once.

Remittance quality depends on supplier data

The agent can only route remittance in the format a supplier's AR system requires if that preference is known and current. Building this dataset requires either direct supplier integration or inference from prior successful cash applications — both of which take time to accumulate at scale.

Optimisation value compounds with data volume

Payment timing and modality optimisation models calibrate to a customer's specific patterns over time. A new customer starts with lower confidence and broader exception rates than a customer with twelve months of transaction history. Expectation-setting at onboarding matters.

Conclusion

The harder problem is the better opportunity

Invoice capture is a more tractable AI problem. Payment orchestration is a more valuable one. The complexity that makes it difficult to build is the same complexity that makes it difficult to replicate. An agent that executes payments suboptimally is worse than no agent at all — the stakes of getting it wrong are immediate and financial. That raises the bar for what the infrastructure underneath must provide.

Centime's position — bank connectivity, real-time cash data, verified supplier accounts, multi-rail payment access, and distribution through the bank channel — is not incidental to this capability. It is the capability. The agent is the decision layer on top of infrastructure that takes years to assemble.

The payment routing logic is the surface. The bank relationships, cash intelligence, verified supplier network, and payment workflow entitlement are what make it work. Competitors need all of them. Most will not get there.
