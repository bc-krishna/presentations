Section 2

The Market Moment

2.1 The Fintech Threat to Commercial Banking

Over the past five years, a new category of fintech platform has emerged that directly threatens banks’ commercial customer relationships. Platforms like Brex, Ramp, and BILL combine corporate cards, expense management, AP automation, and bill pay into integrated experiences that bypass traditional banks entirely. These platforms target the same mid-market CFOs and controllers that are banks’ most valuable commercial clients.

The threat is structural, not cyclical. These platforms consolidate spend visibility, payment execution, and financial controls into a single interface. Once a company moves its expense management and AP to Ramp, for example, the bank’s relationship thins to a commoditized deposit and payment rail. The bank loses visibility, loses stickiness, and eventually risks losing the operating account altogether.

2.2 The Capital One / Brex Acquisition

On January 22, 2026, Capital One announced it would acquire Brex for approximately $5.15 billion in cash and stock. The deal is expected to close mid-2026. This is a watershed moment for the industry:

  • Validation of the category: A top-10 US bank paid $5B+ for a spend management platform. This signals that banks view integrated spend management as strategically critical, not a nice-to-have.
  • Competitive escalation: Capital One now has a modern spend management stack to pair with its massive card infrastructure. Every other commercial bank must respond or risk losing competitive ground.
  • Brex neutralization: Brex was the leading independent fintech threatening banks’ commercial card programs. It is now a bank-owned asset. This changes the competitive dynamic—but it doesn’t eliminate the fintech threat, because Ramp remains independent and is growing aggressively.
  • Build-vs-buy signal: Capital One’s decision to acquire rather than build internally (at a $5B+ price tag) tells every mid-market bank that building this capability from scratch is unrealistic. Partnership is the pragmatic path.

2.3 Ramp’s Accelerating Growth

While Brex heads into Capital One’s orbit, Ramp has emerged as the dominant independent spend management platform. Ramp is trusted by over 330 publicly traded companies including Visa, CBRE, and Shopify, and claims to have helped more than 50,000 businesses save $10 billion. Over 4,200 companies have switched directly from Brex to Ramp.

Ramp’s positioning is explicitly anti-bank: fast feature velocity, no legacy constraints, AI-driven automation, and a focus on cost savings for the customer rather than interchange revenue for the issuer. Ramp’s independence is now a core marketing message—directly contrasting itself against Brex’s new bank ownership.

For mid-market banks, Ramp represents the more dangerous long-term competitor. It is not being acquired. It is actively pursuing bank customers. And it is positioning bank ownership as a disadvantage.

2.4 The Window of Opportunity

12–18 month window

The months following the Brex acquisition represent a unique window. Banks are newly aware of the threat. Brex customers face uncertainty about product direction under Capital One. And no white-label, bank-distributed alternative exists that covers the full spend management stack. A Centime + InLogik partnership can fill this gap before the market consolidates further.

Section 3

The Gap: Why Banks Need a Partner

3.1 What Fintechs Offer (That Banks Don’t)

Brex, Ramp, and BILL have converged on a remarkably similar value proposition: a single platform that handles corporate cards, expense management, AP automation, bill pay, and spend analytics.

  • Unified experience: One login, one dashboard, one set of controls across all spend categories.
  • Real-time visibility: CFOs can see card spend, invoice obligations, and cash position in a single view.
  • AI-driven automation: Auto-coding of expenses, policy enforcement at point of swipe, duplicate detection, and anomaly flagging.
  • Deep ERP integration: Direct connections to NetSuite, Sage Intacct, QuickBooks, and increasingly Oracle and Workday.
  • Speed to value: Customers can be live in days, not months.

3.2 What Banks Currently Offer

Most mid-market banks cobble together a commercial offering from disconnected components: a commercial card program managed through a processor-provided portal, treasury management services for payments, and perhaps a referral relationship with an AP automation vendor.

What fintechs deliver

One platform for all spend management. AI-driven automation. Seamless ERP integration. Live in days.

What banks currently offer

Fragmented tools. Multiple logins. Manual processes. Third-party branding. Each component individually replaceable.

3.3 The Build-vs-Partner Decision

Banks face three options: build internally, acquire, or partner. Building a modern spend management platform from scratch requires deep software engineering talent that banks struggle to attract, multi-year timelines, and significant capital investment—Capital One’s $5.15B acquisition of Brex benchmarks the replacement cost. Acquiring a fintech is prohibitively expensive for most mid-market banks and comes with integration risk.

Partnership is the pragmatic path. The right partners bring proven, white-label technology that can be deployed under the bank’s brand quickly, with the bank retaining its customer relationships and revenue streams.

Section 7

The Bank Value Proposition

7.1 Drive Card Interchange Revenue

Card interchange is one of the most profitable revenue streams in commercial banking. Every time a bank’s commercial card is used for a business expense—travel, supplies, subscriptions, dining—the bank earns interchange. But interchange revenue depends on two things: card issuance and card usage.

InLogik’s card management platform makes it easier for banks to issue and manage commercial card programs at scale. ExpenseMe drives card adoption by making the expense workflow seamless—when expensing is easy, employees use cards instead of personal reimbursements or manual processes. Centime’s virtual card capabilities within Integrated Payables extend card usage to vendor payments, generating interchange on transactions that would otherwise flow through ACH or check.

The combined effect: more cards issued, more transactions on cards, more interchange revenue for the bank.

7.2 Retain and Grow Commercial Deposits

When a company’s AP and payment operations run through a bank-branded platform, the operating account stays at the bank. Payment funding, payroll-adjacent expense reimbursements, and cash management all flow through the bank’s rails. Centime’s cash flow forecasting further deepens this relationship by giving the bank’s commercial customers a reason to consolidate cash visibility with their primary bank.

Without an integrated platform, these deposits are at risk. When a company moves to Ramp or Brex, payment funding follows. The bank retains a declining checking account while the fintech captures the full payment flow.

7.3 Compete Without Building

The combined platform allows banks to offer a comprehensive spend management solution without the multi-year, multi-million dollar investment of building one internally. Both Centime and InLogik operate as white-label platforms, meaning the bank can present the solution as its own—consistent with its brand, integrated into its digital banking experience, and supported through its existing relationship management model.

This is particularly relevant for mid-market banks in the $10B–$100B asset range that lack the technology budgets of a JPMorgan or Capital One. Partnership delivers the capability at a fraction of the cost and time.

7.4 Deepen Customer Relationships

Daily engagement creates switching costs

A bank offering spend management becomes embedded in its commercial customers’ daily financial operations. Controllers and CFOs log into the bank’s platform every day to approve invoices, review expenses, and check cash positions. This daily engagement creates switching costs, generates data that enables advisory conversations, and positions the bank as a strategic partner rather than a commodity service provider.

Section 9

Go-to-Market Strategy

9.1 Joint Bank-Channel Distribution

The primary GTM motion is joint engagement with US banks. Centime brings an established pipeline of 6 active US bank partnerships, a leadership team with 30+ bank relationships built at MineralTree, and the institutional knowledge of how banks evaluate and adopt embedded fintech solutions. InLogik brings card management expertise and a track record with major banks globally that adds credibility to the combined pitch.

The conversation with banks centers on a simple premise: you need a complete spend management platform to compete with Ramp and the Capital One/Brex combination. You can’t build it. We’ve already built the pieces. Together, we deliver the whole thing—white-labeled, under your brand, driving your interchange and deposit revenue.

9.2 Phased Approach

Phase 1 — Product Mapping

Detailed technical assessment of integration points between Centime and InLogik platforms. Identify the combined user experience, data flows, and API touchpoints. Define the joint product offering.

Phase 2 — Pilot Bank

Select one existing Centime bank partner to pilot the combined offering. This provides a reference case and validates the joint platform in a live bank environment.

Phase 3 — Formalized Partnership

Based on pilot results, establish formal partnership terms, pricing models, and joint go-to-market materials. Begin co-selling to additional bank prospects.

Phase 4 — Scale

Expand across both companies’ bank networks. Centime opens InLogik’s entry into US mid-market banks. InLogik opens Centime’s access to international banks and card networks.

9.3 Revenue Model Considerations

The partnership economics should align with how banks buy: the bank pays for the platform (likely a per-customer or per-transaction fee) and offers it to commercial customers as part of their banking relationship. Revenue sharing between Centime and InLogik follows the product boundary—each company earns on its own product modules.

Card interchange is a critical economic driver. InLogik’s card management and expense platform drives card usage, which generates interchange for the bank. Centime’s virtual card payments within Integrated Payables generate additional interchange on vendor payments. The bank is incentivized to promote the full platform because every module drives revenue.
