Centime Exchange

Why we position it the way we do. Not the pitch. The reasoning.
February 2026 · Internal
The Question

Is Exchange a product or a feature?

It's both, and the way we talk about it depends on who's in the room. But behind the scenes, we need to be precise — because positioning choices affect pricing, packaging, sales motion, and how our bank partners present us. This document lays out the reasoning so everyone on the team argues from the same set of facts.

The Cautionary Tale

Standalone supplier management has a ceiling

PaymentWorks

The Facts

Founded 2013. Roughly $12M in revenue after twelve years. Around 100 employees. A J.P. Morgan partnership that validates the category but hasn't changed the growth curve. They do one thing — supplier onboarding and verification — and they do it well.

The Lesson

Good Product, Small Market

PaymentWorks proves the capability matters. It also proves that selling supplier onboarding as a standalone product creates a low ceiling. The buyer values it, but not enough to build a large business around it. The category is real. The standalone economics are not.

Why Pure-Play Doesn't Scale

Four structural problems we'd inherit

Problem 01

Onboarding is a feature, not a destination

Nobody logs in every morning to onboard suppliers. It's a task that happens when needed, not a daily workflow. Products that get used occasionally have weaker retention and lower willingness to pay.

Problem 02

The buyer doesn't wake up thinking about it

CFOs and AP leaders think about cash, payments, and reconciliation every day. Supplier onboarding surfaces when there's a problem — a new vendor, a compliance gap, a fraud scare. Reactive demand is harder to sell into than daily pain.

Problem 03

There's no lock-in

Supplier data is portable. If a competitor offers onboarding bundled with their AP platform, switching costs are low. There's no transaction stream, no network effect, no compounding data asset that makes leaving painful.

Problem 04

The network never materializes

Every supplier management company pitches a network: "onboard once, share everywhere." It never works. Buyers want their own verification standards. Suppliers don't trust shared networks. The promised efficiency doesn't arrive.

What We've Built

Exchange has everything PaymentWorks has — and more

Real-time bank account ownership verification. Configurable outreach campaigns with automated follow-up. Comprehensive document management with automated parsing and expiration tracking. Mobile-responsive supplier registration that works without a login. Industry-specific document categories. Tiered onboarding workflows that scale from a quick bank verification to a full compliance package.

The capability is strong. The question was never whether we could compete with PaymentWorks on features. The question is whether we should compete with them on positioning. If we sold Exchange standalone, we'd inherit all four structural problems. So we don't.

The Strategic Play

Exchange is what makes Integrated Payables win

Every AP platform handles invoices and payments — that's table stakes. None have solved what happens before the first invoice: verifying the supplier, collecting their banking details, confirming their tax and compliance documents, and creating a clean master record. That gap is where errors start, where fraud enters, and where the whole downstream process breaks.

Exchange fills that gap. It turns Integrated Payables from "another AP automation tool" into something structurally different. The pitch writes itself: "We're the only platform that starts before the first invoice and runs through the last payment." That's not a tagline. That's an architectural advantage that no Tier 2 competitor can replicate without building what we've already built.

The Economics

The front door vs. the house

Exchange standalone might command $3,500 per year — a respectable SaaS contract but a small one. The value isn't in the subscription. It's in what Exchange unlocks downstream.

Exchange standalone — supplier onboarding and verification ~$3,500
Integrated Payables base — AP automation, approvals, GL sync $8K–$12K
Transaction revenue — virtual card rebates, ACH fees, cross-border $5K–$18K+
Full stack per customer — Exchange + IP + transactions $15K–$30K+

PaymentWorks has the front door but no house behind it. We have the house. Exchange is the front door that gets people inside.

Distribution

The bank channel only works with the full stack

Banks will not white-label standalone supplier onboarding. The value proposition is too narrow, the revenue share too small, and the integration effort not worth it for a feature that doesn't drive commercial payment volume. They will white-label integrated payables — because it drives commercial card adoption, increases deposit activity, and gives their treasury sales team something tangible to sell.

No Tier 2 AP competitor distributes through bank channels. Not Tipalti, not Stampli, not AvidXchange at our scale. That is our structural advantage, and it only exists because we offer the full stack: onboard the supplier, process the invoice, execute the payment. Exchange is what makes the first step credible to a bank's compliance team.

Take Exchange out of the bundle and the bank channel advantage disappears. Keep it in, and we have a distribution moat that no pure-play competitor can cross.

The Answer

A named capability with a standalone option

When we sell Integrated Payables

Exchange is included. One product, one price, one contract. We don't itemize it, we don't charge separately, and we don't give the buyer a reason to think they can skip it. It's the supplier layer of the platform — the reason everything downstream works cleanly.

When someone only needs enrollment

Exchange is available standalone. Priced modestly. Positioned as a foot in the door. Every standalone Exchange customer is a future IP upsell — they've already onboarded their suppliers on our platform. The switching cost to our full stack drops to near zero.

The bottom line: we don't lead with standalone Exchange. We lead with Integrated Payables and let Exchange be the differentiator that closes the deal. Standalone is the exception, not the strategy.

The Competitive Moat

Fraud indemnification creates a flywheel

Bundle indemnification into the base Integrated Payables price. Every payment to an Exchange-verified supplier is indemnified up to a defined limit. The customer doesn't pay extra. They don't file claims. They pay a vendor through our platform, and if that vendor turns out to be fraudulent, we cover the loss. No Tier 2 competitor can match this because none of them own the verification layer.

Better verification → lower fraud risk → higher indemnification limits → stronger sales positioning → more customers → more suppliers verified → better data → better verification

This is the flywheel. Each turn makes the next turn easier. Indemnification is not a feature — it's the commercial expression of Exchange's verification quality. It turns a technical capability into a financial guarantee that procurement teams and CFOs understand immediately.

Go-to-Market

80/20: IP-led with Exchange featured

Eighty percent of our sales and marketing materials lead with Integrated Payables. Exchange appears as the featured capability that differentiates us — the reason a buyer picks Centime over Stampli or Tipalti. It gets a named section in every deck, a dedicated page on the website, and a clear explanation of why starting before the first invoice matters.

Twenty percent of materials are Exchange-standalone. These target a specific use case: companies that aren't ready for full AP automation but need to solve supplier onboarding and verification now. The standalone materials are designed to land the account and create the conditions for an IP upsell within twelve months.

Internally, the sales team gets competitive battle cards against PaymentWorks and Trustpair. These are not customer-facing. They exist so our reps can handle objections when a prospect says "we're also looking at a dedicated supplier management tool." The answer is always: we do everything they do, and we do everything that comes after.
