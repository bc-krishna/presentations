The Ask

Reinstate B1230FC with amended coverage

We held a Financial Institutions Bond (Agreement A) covering unauthorized electronic funds transfer. It lapsed — we couldn't justify the premium with no recovery mechanism and zero claims. Since then, we built Centime Exchange, a supplier verification platform that fundamentally changes the risk profile.

We're proposing to reinstate the bond and add a new Agreement (B) covering social engineering and vendor impersonation fraud — the category the original bond never addressed, and the one that causes the most damage. Coverage applies only to payments routed to Exchange-verified suppliers.

The Coverage Gap

System breach is covered. Social engineering is not.

Agreement A — UEFT

Covers unauthorized electronic funds transfer: someone hacks into the system and initiates a payment without authorization. $5M aggregate, $100K single loss, $10K deductible, $30K annual premium. Zero claims filed during the active period.

The gap — BEC

Does not cover business email compromise or supplier impersonation. In BEC, the buyer authorizes the transfer — they're just deceived about the destination. The payment is "authorized" by policy definition, so the bond doesn't respond. This is where the actual losses happen.

The bond covered the scenario that didn't happen. The scenario that happens every day was never covered.

The Threat

BEC is the #1 cybercrime category

$2.7B+
Annual losses reported to FBI — business email compromise

The attack is simple: an adversary impersonates a supplier and sends updated banking details. The AP team processes the change. The next payment goes to a fraudulent account. By the time anyone notices, the money is gone.

Median mid-market loss per incident: $50K–$125K. The buyer authorized every step. No system was breached. No credential was stolen. The vulnerability is the process, not the perimeter.

Traditional fidelity bonds and cyber policies exclude this scenario because the transfer was "authorized." That's the gap we're asking to close.

What's Changed

Centime Exchange closes the primary attack vector

Since the bond lapsed, Centime built Exchange — a supplier verification and onboarding platform. The critical capability is CAS: Centime Account Services.

CAS

Real-time bank account ownership verification

Browser-based bank authentication that proves account ownership in real time. Not micro-deposits (days to settle, easily spoofed). Not database lookups (stale data). Real-time proof that the person submitting bank details owns the account.

Net Effect

The BEC vector is closed

For Exchange-verified suppliers, the primary BEC attack — substituting a fraudulent bank account — cannot succeed. The adversary would need to pass real-time bank authentication on an account they don't own. That's the verification standard no other platform matches.
