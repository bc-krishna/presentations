We are writing to propose the reinstatement and amendment of Financial Institutions Bond B1230FC, previously underwritten by Certain Underwriters at Lloyd’s on behalf of Centime, Inc.

Centime is a financial process automation company that provides mid-market finance teams with an integrated suite of solutions — accounts payable automation, accounts receivable automation, expense management, business banking, and cash flow forecasting — embedded directly within their ERP systems (NetSuite, Sage Intacct, QuickBooks, and Microsoft Dynamics).

On the payables side, Centime offers two complementary solutions: AP Automation (invoice-to-pay, covering invoice capture, coding, PO matching, approvals, and supplier management) and Payments Automation (integrated payables, covering payment execution via ACH, virtual card, and check, with reconciliation back to the ERP).

The coverage requested in this letter applies to payments made through either payables solution.

This letter provides context on the existing policy, the gap between what it covers and the risk we need to insure against, the competitive landscape that creates urgency for this coverage, and a specific proposal for amended terms. We recognize this is a more detailed submission than is typical for a reinstatement request, but the strategic context is important to understanding both the opportunity for the underwriter and the risk profile we bring to the table.

01 — The Existing Policy

What the bond covers

Bond B1230FC is a Financial Institutions Bond with a single insuring agreement covering Unauthorized Electronic Funds Transfer (UEFT).

The insuring agreement covers loss resulting directly from an Insured having transferred, paid, or delivered any Money as the direct result of an Unauthorized Electronic Fund Transfer while using the Centime system as authorized for Check, ACH, or credit card payments.

The definition of “Unauthorized Electronic Funds Transfer” is specific: an Electronic Funds Transfer from an Account initiated by a person other than the Insured without the actual authority to initiate the transfer and from which the Insured receives no benefit, and which arises out of a failure or violation of the security of an Insured’s Computer System.

This is a system-breach coverage. It covers the scenario in which a bad actor compromises a customer’s computer system, gains unauthorized access to the Centime platform, and initiates a fraudulent payment. The customer did not authorize the transfer. The transfer arose from a security failure of the customer’s systems.

Claims History

The policy performed exactly as designed. During the period it was in force, Centime had zero claims. The controls embedded in our platform — multi-factor authentication, role-based access, payment approval workflows — prevented the covered loss scenario from occurring.

02 — The Coverage Gap

What the policy does not cover

The fastest-growing category of B2B payment fraud is not system breach. It is supplier impersonation and business email compromise (BEC).

In these scenarios, no system is breached. No unauthorized access occurs. Instead, a bad actor impersonates a legitimate supplier — or compromises a supplier’s email account — and submits fraudulent banking information to the buyer. The buyer, acting in good faith on what it believes is verified supplier data, initiates a payment to the wrong account. The buyer authorized the transfer. They were simply deceived about the destination.

This is the fraud vector that the current bond explicitly does not cover. The UEFT definition requires that the transfer be “initiated by a person other than the Insured without the actual authority” and that it “arise out of a failure or violation of the security of an Insured’s Computer System.” In a BEC/vendor impersonation scenario, neither condition is met.

Covered (Agreement A)

  • Bad actor hacks customer system
  • Gains unauthorized access to Centime
  • Initiates payment without customer knowledge
  • Customer did not authorize the transfer

Not Covered (The Gap)

  • Bad actor impersonates a supplier
  • Submits fraudulent bank details
  • Customer authorizes payment in good faith
  • No system breach occurred

The scale of the problem

Business email compromise is the single largest category of cybercrime loss in the United States. The FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) has consistently reported BEC losses exceeding $2.7 billion annually, dwarfing ransomware, data breaches, and traditional wire fraud combined. The median BEC loss for a mid-market company is approximately $50,000–$125,000 per incident, with tail events reaching seven figures.

The reason is structural: traditional AP processes rely on supplier-provided banking information that is verified through manual processes — voided checks, email confirmations, phone callbacks to numbers the supplier provided. These controls are routinely defeated by sophisticated social engineering. The buyer’s AP team believes it has verified the account. It has not.

This is the risk our platform is specifically designed to mitigate, and the risk we need insurance to backstop.

03 — What Has Changed

Centime Exchange — Supplier Verification Layer

Since the bond lapsed, Centime has developed and deployed a capability called Centime Exchange, which is a supplier enrollment and verification platform that fundamentally changes the risk profile for the fraud vector described above.

Exchange is a supplier onboarding and management platform that serves as the verification layer for Centime’s payables solutions — both AP Automation and Payments Automation. Before a supplier can receive any payment through the Centime platform, it must complete Exchange verification. The key controls:

  • Real-Time Bank Account Ownership Verification. Our proprietary account verification technology verifies bank account ownership in real time through browser-based bank authentication. The supplier logs into their own bank account through a secure session, and we confirm that the account number provided matches an account to which the supplier has authenticated access. This is not a micro-deposit. It is not a third-party database lookup. It is real-time proof of ownership.
  • Comprehensive Document Collection and Verification. Exchange collects and validates supplier documentation across three tiers of increasing scrutiny: Micro/Individual suppliers (W-9, basic business verification), Domestic Business suppliers (W-9, Articles of Incorporation, Certificate of Good Standing, insurance certificates with automated parsing and expiration tracking), and International suppliers (W-8 series, SWIFT/IBAN validation).
  • KYB and Compliance Screening. Exchange performs Secretary of State verification and TIN validation through Middesk, and AML/sanctions/PEP screening through Didit (OFAC SDN, UN, EU, and 1,300+ global watchlists with real-time checks and ongoing daily monitoring). Available per-supplier and can be made mandatory at any verification tier.
  • Configurable Outreach and Enrollment Campaigns. Automated outreach with configurable follow-up sequences, deadline-driven enrollment windows, and real-time completion tracking. This drives enrollment velocity — the percentage of a buyer’s supplier base that has been verified — which directly correlates to the percentage of payments flowing to verified accounts.
  • Mobile-Responsive Supplier Portal. Suppliers complete registration, upload documents, and manage their profiles from any device. Mobile access increases completion rates, which increases verification coverage, which reduces the residual unverified population.

The net effect: when a payment is made through the Centime platform to an Exchange-verified supplier, the bank account receiving that payment has been proven to belong to the supplier through real-time ownership verification. The primary attack vector for BEC/vendor impersonation fraud — substituting a fraudulent bank account for a legitimate one — is closed.

This is a materially stronger risk posture than existed when the original bond was written. The original bond covered system breach risk with platform-level access controls. We are now asking to extend coverage to supplier impersonation risk with a dedicated verification infrastructure that did not exist at the time of original underwriting.

04 — Competitive Context

Why this coverage matters

We are not requesting this coverage solely as a risk transfer mechanism. Fraud indemnification has become a competitive requirement in the B2B payments market.

PaymentWorks: The benchmark

PaymentWorks is a supplier onboarding platform that has been in market since 2013. They serve approximately 200 customers, primarily in higher education and public sector, with estimated annual revenue of $12M. Their marquee relationship is with J.P. Morgan.

PaymentWorks’ primary competitive differentiator is not their software. It is their fraud indemnification. They offer coverage of up to $2 million per fraudulent payment, included automatically in every customer contract. No separate purchase decision. No opt-in. It is bundled.

Their sales motion leads with this. In competitive evaluations, particularly with procurement committees and risk-conscious finance teams, the indemnification is frequently the deciding factor. The software capabilities — supplier onboarding, document collection, bank account validation — are table stakes. The insurance is the close.

Centime’s distribution model

Centime distributes through bank partnerships. Banks white-label Centime’s payables platform and offer it to their commercial banking clients.

Bank partners are highly receptive to fraud indemnification as a differentiator. It strengthens their commercial banking relationship, reduces the risk of payment fraud flowing through their rails, and gives their relationship managers a concrete value proposition when presenting AP automation to commercial clients.

No Tier 2 AP platform distributes through bank channels. This is a structural advantage for Centime, and fraud indemnification makes the bank channel pitch significantly stronger.

05 — The Proposal

Our proposal

We are requesting reinstatement of Bond B1230FC with the following amendments.

5a. Amended insuring agreement

We propose adding a second insuring agreement — Insuring Agreement (B) — to cover loss resulting from Social Engineering and Vendor Impersonation fraud. This would sit alongside the existing Insuring Agreement (A) for Unauthorized Electronic Funds Transfer.

The new insuring agreement would cover:

  • Loss resulting directly from an Insured having transferred, paid, or delivered any Money to a person, account, or entity that is not the intended recipient, where such transfer was induced by fraudulent instruction, fraudulent impersonation of a supplier, or fraudulent alteration of banking or payment routing information
  • Provided that the payment was made through the Centime platform (via either AP Automation or Payments Automation) to a supplier that had been enrolled and verified through the Centime Exchange platform at the time of the transfer

The critical underwriting control: coverage applies only to payments made to Exchange-verified suppliers. This means every covered payment has gone through real-time bank account ownership verification, document collection and validation, and any applicable KYB/compliance screening. The underwriter’s exposure is limited to the population of payments where Centime’s full verification stack has been applied. Verified = covered. Unverified = not covered.

5b. Proposed limits

Agreement (A): UEFT Agreement (B): Social Engineering
Aggregate LimitUSD 5,000,000USD 5,000,000
Single Loss LimitUSD 100,000USD 500,000
DeductibleUSD 10,000USD 25,000

We recognize that Agreement (B) represents a different risk profile than Agreement (A) and have proposed a higher single loss limit ($500,000 vs. $100,000) with a correspondingly higher deductible ($25,000 vs. $10,000) to reflect both the typical loss severity for vendor impersonation incidents and Centime’s willingness to retain meaningful first-dollar exposure.

We are open to discussion on these figures. Our competitive requirement is a single loss limit that is credible in the market — high enough to be meaningful in sales conversations with mid-market finance teams. The $500,000 figure is our starting position; we would welcome the underwriter’s perspective on the appropriate limit given the Exchange verification controls described above.

5c. Premium and funding structure

We are proposing a structure in which Centime acts as the distribution channel for a master insurance facility, rather than purchasing a traditional lump-sum annual policy.

How this would work: Centime and the underwriter agree on a master facility that covers all Centime payables customers — whether using AP Automation, Payments Automation, or both — who meet the Exchange verification requirements. The premium is calculated on a per-customer basis and scales automatically as Centime’s customer base grows. Centime collects the per-customer premium through its platform billing and remits to the underwriter on an agreed schedule (monthly or quarterly).

  • No lump-sum premium outlay. Premium is funded by the customers it covers, collected through existing billing infrastructure, and remitted to the underwriter. Self-funding from day one.
  • Predictable, growing premium stream. Per-customer premium grows automatically as Centime adds customers and bank partners. Currently 150+ customers across six banks; projecting 250+ by end of 2026.
  • Automatic enrollment, no adverse selection. Every payables customer is enrolled automatically. No opt-in decision eliminates adverse selection risk.
  • Alignment of incentives. Centime is incentivized to drive Exchange enrollment velocity, which simultaneously reduces the underwriter’s residual risk exposure.

06 — The Underwriter’s Perspective

Why this is an attractive risk

We want to be direct about why we believe this is an attractive risk for the underwriter, not just a good outcome for Centime.

Superior verification controls

Most B2B payment platforms rely on third-party database lookups, micro-deposits, or manual document review. Centime’s account verification technology performs real-time bank account ownership verification through direct bank authentication — a fundamentally stronger control than anything else in market.

Binary, auditable trigger

A supplier is either Exchange-verified or it is not. This is a system state that is timestamped, logged, and auditable. The underwriter can define minimum verification requirements and Centime enforces them programmatically.

Self-funding, no adverse selection

Every payables customer contributes to the premium pool automatically. No opt-in eliminates adverse selection. Premium income grows as the customer base grows. Low-friction, predictable revenue stream for the underwriter.

Clean claims history

Zero fraudulent payment losses since inception. The prior bond period produced zero claims. Platform controls and customer base represent a well-managed risk.

The competitive precedent exists. PaymentWorks has offered bundled fraud indemnification to its customer base for years, also through specialty insurance underwriting. Their model has proven commercially viable and, to our knowledge, has not produced significant claims activity. The market has established that B2B payment platform indemnification is an insurable risk when backed by appropriate verification controls. Centime’s verification controls, particularly real-time bank account verification, are more rigorous than PaymentWorks’ validation methods.
