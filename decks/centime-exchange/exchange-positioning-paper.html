Why We Position Exchange the Way We Do

The reasoning behind our positioning choices for Centime Exchange — how it fits within our Financial Process Automation platform (AP Automation and Integrated Payables), why standalone supplier management hasn't broken out, and what that means for pricing, packaging, and distribution.

01 — The Core Question

The question we keep coming back to

Central Question

Is Centime Exchange a product or a feature?

We've gone back and forth on this. The honest answer is that it's both, and the way we talk about it depends on who's in the room. But behind the scenes, we need to be precise about why we've landed where we've landed — because the positioning choices we make here affect pricing, packaging, sales motion, and how our bank partners present us.

This document is the reasoning. Not the pitch. The reasoning.

02 — What Centime Is

What Centime is

Centime is a Financial Process Automation platform. Two core solutions cover the full invoice-to-payment lifecycle. Exchange — supplier enrollment and verification — complements both.

AP Automation (Invoice-to-Pay)

  • Invoice capture and data extraction
  • GL coding and cost allocation
  • Approval workflows
  • ERP posting and sync

Payments Automation (Integrated Payables)

  • Payment execution — ACH, virtual card, check, wire
  • Supplier payment method optimization
  • Remittance delivery
  • Payment reconciliation

Together, AP Automation and Integrated Payables cover everything from the moment an invoice arrives to the moment a payment clears. Exchange sits in front of both: suppliers are enrolled, verified, and documented before AP Automation processes their first invoice and before Integrated Payables executes their first payment.

03 — Market Context

The state of standalone supplier management

There is a category called "supplier onboarding" or "vendor management." It exists. Companies buy it. But it has never produced a breakout company.

PaymentWorks is the clearest example. Founded in 2013. Roughly $12M in annual revenue after twelve years. About 100 employees. Never raised a real growth round. They found a niche — higher-ed, public sector, enterprise compliance buyers — and they've stayed there. J.P. Morgan is their marquee partnership. They do one thing well: collect vendor data, validate it, and push it into an ERP. They've added fraud indemnification (up to $2M per payment) and early pay, but the core is still the same: clean up the vendor master file.

PaymentWorks after 12 years: ~$12M annual revenue. ~100 employees. No real growth round. One marquee bank partner (J.P. Morgan). One niche (higher-ed, public sector, enterprise compliance). Not failing. Not scaling.

Why pure-play supplier onboarding hasn't broken out

Four structural reasons:

1. Onboarding is a feature, not a destination

Every AP automation platform eventually adds basic supplier onboarding. Tipalti has it. BILL has network effects that approximate it. Stampli launched Advanced Vendor Management. The moment onboarding becomes table stakes inside broader platforms, the standalone product loses its reason to exist as a separate purchase.

2. The buyer doesn't wake up thinking about it

A CFO or AP director opens their laptop thinking "I need to pay my vendors." They are not thinking "I need a supplier onboarding platform." Onboarding is a means to an end. The end is payments. If you sell the means without the end, you're always a secondary purchase.

3. There's no lock-in

Once you've onboarded your suppliers, what keeps you on the platform? The data has already moved to your ERP. The W-9s are filed. The bank accounts are verified. There's no ongoing transaction flow that makes switching painful.

4. The network never materializes

Every supplier onboarding platform pitches a network effect — "once a supplier registers with us, they're pre-verified for every buyer on the platform." In practice, this barely works. Every buyer has different document requirements, different compliance thresholds, different insurance minimums. The customization kills the network.

04 — Exchange Capabilities

What this means for Exchange

Exchange has every capability that PaymentWorks has — and in most areas, more. If we wanted to, we could go head-to-head as a standalone supplier management platform and win on features. But we'd inherit all four structural problems.

  • Real-time bank account ownership verification — not micro-deposits
  • Configurable outreach campaigns — not a single email invite
  • Comprehensive document management — automated parsing, metadata extraction, and expiration tracking
  • Mobile-responsive supplier registration — suppliers complete onboarding from any device
  • Industry-specific document category groups — tailored requirements by vertical
  • Tiered onboarding — based on supplier complexity and risk profile
05 — The Core Argument

The play: Exchange is what makes Centime's platform win

Centime's Financial Process Automation platform — AP Automation and Integrated Payables — is the product. Exchange is what makes it better than every other platform on the market.

Every AP automation platform — Tipalti, BILL, Stampli, AvidXchange — handles invoice processing and payment execution. Those are table stakes. None of them have solved what happens before the first invoice and before the first payment. How does a supplier get into the system? How do you verify their bank account before AP Automation can process their invoices? How do you collect the right documents? How do you confirm payment details before Integrated Payables can execute? How do you run outreach campaigns? How do you track insurance expirations?

That's Exchange. It fills the gap before the first invoice (serving AP Automation) and before the first payment (serving Integrated Payables). No one else is closing either gap.

"We're the only platform that starts before the first invoice and runs through the last payment. Your suppliers are verified, documented, and transaction-ready before a single dollar moves."

The revenue math confirms it

  • $3,500 base platform fee
  • AP Automation subscription revenue — invoice capture, coding, approval workflows, posting
  • Payment processing revenue — ACH, virtual card, check
  • Virtual card rebates back to the buyer
  • Early Pay / dynamic discounting fees
  • Document management and compliance add-ons
  • Per-supplier KYB (Middesk) and AML/sanctions screening (Didit)

A fully realized AP + IP + Exchange relationship generates $15K–$30K+ per customer annually. The onboarding fee is the front door. The transactions are the house.

This is why PaymentWorks is stuck at $12M. They have the front door but no house behind it.

The ad valorem thesis

Here's a hypothesis we haven't fully proven yet but believe is directionally right: Exchange may be most valuable as a driver of ad valorem transactions — dynamic discounting and virtual card payments — where Centime earns a percentage of every dollar that moves.

Exchange verifies a supplier's bank account, collects their payment preferences, and confirms they're transaction-ready. That's the foundation for enrolling suppliers into dynamic discounting programs and virtual card acceptance. Without verified enrollment, those programs don't launch. With it, the buyer can offer early pay terms or route payments to virtual cards from day one.

This probably matters more for IP customers than AP customers. AP Automation generates subscription revenue regardless of payment method. But IP revenue is transaction-driven, and the highest-margin transactions are virtual card (rebate revenue) and dynamic discounting (discount share). Exchange is the enrollment engine that feeds those programs.

We haven't proven this yet. The data will come as we scale platform customers and measure which transaction types Exchange-verified suppliers adopt. But if the thesis holds, Exchange isn't just a competitive differentiator — it's the revenue accelerator for our highest-margin products.

Distribution: direct and bank

We sell two ways: direct to mid-market finance teams, and through bank partners — FNBO, and others in the pipeline. Exchange strengthens both motions differently.

In direct sales, Exchange is the differentiator that separates Centime from every other AP and payments platform in the demo. When a prospect is evaluating Tipalti, BILL, Stampli, and Centime side by side, Exchange is the capability none of the others can match. "We start before the first invoice" is a positioning statement only Centime can make.

In bank-channel sales, Exchange amplifies the economics. Banks will not white-label standalone supplier onboarding. But they will white-label a financial process automation platform that drives commercial payment volume through their rails. The supplier enrollment layer is what makes the platform defensible; the payment volume is what makes the bank say yes.

No Tier 2 AP competitor distributes through bank channels. Our dual distribution — direct sales where Exchange differentiates, plus bank channels where Exchange amplifies — is a structural advantage that compounds as we add partners.

06 — The Answer

So is Exchange a product or a feature?

It's a named capability of the Centime platform — with a standalone option for buyers who need it separately.

When we sell the Centime platform

  • Exchange is included
  • It's the enrollment foundation for both AP and IP
  • One platform, one price, one contract
  • 80% of materials and positioning

When someone only needs enrollment

  • Exchange is available standalone
  • This is a foot in the door
  • Eventual full platform upsell (AP + IP)
  • 20% of materials and positioning

But we don't lead with standalone Exchange. Exchange has a name because the enrollment capability needs an identity that travels — in competitive conversations, in bank partner materials, in customer-facing documentation.

07 — Indemnification (Exploratory)

The fraud indemnification question

One more piece worth exploring — though we haven't committed to it and it's unclear whether we can offer it as part of Exchange.

We previously held a specialty insurance policy that indemnified customers against loss from fraudulent payments to Exchange-verified suppliers. It lapsed because we couldn't justify the $30K annual premium when we had no mechanism to recover the cost.

PaymentWorks offers something similar — fraud indemnification up to $2M per payment, bundled automatically into their platform. It's not sold separately. It's the thing their sales team leads with, and it's what makes procurement committees comfortable signing off.

In theory, a model could work for us: bundle indemnification into the base platform price, raise the base fee $500–$800 per customer, and across 50+ customers recover the premium. But there are open questions:

  • Can we underwrite this through Exchange specifically, or does it need to attach to the broader platform?
  • Is the $30K premium still accurate, or has our risk profile changed?
  • Do we have the claims infrastructure to support it?
  • Does our zero-claims history help us renegotiate, or has the insurance market moved?
If the answers come back favorably

"Every payment to an Exchange-verified supplier is indemnified up to $X." That's a sentence no Tier 2 AP competitor can match — and the logic is sound, since Exchange verification is exactly the kind of underwriting foundation that lowers insurer risk.

This is worth investigating. It's not part of our current positioning, but it could sharpen it considerably if the economics and logistics work out.

09 — The Bottom Line

The bottom line

We are not building a supplier onboarding company. We are building a financial process automation company — AP Automation and Integrated Payables — with the best supplier enrollment layer in the market.

Exchange is that layer. The business is the platform. The revenue is in invoice processing and payment transactions. The distribution is direct and through banks. Exchange is the engine underneath all of it — the differentiator in direct sales, the amplifier in bank channels, and potentially the accelerator for our highest-margin ad valorem products.
